Impact & Prevention

Prevention begins before impact.

A near-collision unfolds across seconds, not milliseconds. Scroll through the timeline — every stage is a point where a different choice changes the outcome.

T − 5s

Attention state

Driver is scanning the road. Awareness is highest here — this is the safest place to intervene.

T − 4s

Distraction event

A phone notification or conversation briefly pulls visual and cognitive attention off the road.

T − 3s

Hazard appears

A pedestrian, vehicle, or obstacle enters the path. Detection is delayed if attention hasn't returned.

T − 2s

Reaction delay

Perception–reaction time (commonly modeled around ~1–1.5s for alert drivers) begins to elapse.

T − 1s

Brake / steer

Braking force and steering input begin. Vehicle speed and road grip shape how much distance is recovered.

T = 0

Outcome

Whether the vehicle stops in time depends on everything upstream — speed, attention, road condition, and fitness.

Invisible Risks Lab

The risks we rarely talk about.

Click a specimen to open its file. These are systemic and medical factors that rarely make it into everyday road-safety conversation.

Driver medical fitness

Vision, hearing, and cardiovascular health all shape a driver's ability to detect and respond to hazards in time.

Periodic medical screening — especially for commercial and professional drivers — is a recognised element of many road-safety frameworks worldwide, alongside vehicle and road engineering measures.

Sleep, airway & alertness

Untreated sleep-disordered breathing can affect daytime alertness relevant to driving.

The uvula is the small soft-tissue structure visible at the back of the mouth. Airway anatomy is one factor clinicians may consider in a broader assessment of obstructive sleep apnea risk — it is not, on its own, a test of fitness to drive. Severe untreated sleep apnea can cause excessive daytime sleepiness, which is relevant to driving safety and is best evaluated through a proper clinical sleep assessment, not self-diagnosis.

Medication & impairment

Certain medications, alcohol, and drugs slow reaction time and impair judgment.

Many medicine labels carry driving-related warnings. Combining sedating medication with alcohol compounds impairment. Impairment is a leading contributor to crash risk globally.

Fatigue & microsleep

A few seconds of lost awareness at speed can translate into tens of metres of uncontrolled travel.

Explore the full interactive simulator further down this page.

Vehicle condition

Worn tyres, failing brakes, and poor maintenance quietly erode the safety margin every driver relies on.

Routine fitness checks for brakes, tyres, lights, and steering are a basic but often-skipped line of defence.

Road & system design

Blind curves, poor lighting, and missing signage shift responsibility from the individual to the system.

Explore the Road Engineering Lab further down this page for a hands-on view of design-level risk.

Emergency Response Timeline

Outcomes depend on response, too.

Hover each stage of the post-crash chain to see why every link matters.

Crash
The event occurs — the clock for effective response starts immediately.
Detection
Bystanders, connected vehicles, or automated systems recognise something has happened.
Emergency call
A call is placed to emergency services — clarity on what happened matters here.
Location ID
Accurately identifying the location is often one of the biggest delays in rural or highway stretches.
First response
Bystander first aid and scene safety in the first minutes can meaningfully affect outcomes.
Ambulance
Time to a properly equipped ambulance is a key determinant of trauma outcomes.
Trauma care
Access to a trauma-capable facility completes the chain of survival.
